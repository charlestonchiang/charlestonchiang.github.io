Research

We have been involved in a number of past and ongoing medical genetics studies in mapping genetic loci underlying human complex traits. We are also continually interested in extensively investigating the evolutionary forces that shaped the pattern of genetic variability and phenotypic distribution. We are particularly interested in diverse, global human populations. Our successes result from collaborating with innovative colleagues and thriving in resourceful consortiums.


The focus of the group mainly lies in the following areas:

Genetic basis of phenotype in diverse populations

Our group has led past and ongoing medical genetic studies in mapping genetic loci underlying human complex phenotypes such as anthropometric and cardiometabolic traits. Our focus has always been in studying diverse populations with special demographic history, such as African Americans (Chiang et al. PLoS Genet. 2010; Kang*, Chiang*, Palmer* et al. HMG 2010), Sardinians (Zoledziewska*, Sidore*, Chiang*, Sanna* et al. Nat. Genet. 2015), and Finland (Chiang et al. ASHG 2017).

Moving forward, we will be continually interested in understanding the genetic architecture of complex traits in diverse populations, including ongoing collaborations to study cardiometabolic traits using whole-exome or whole-genome data from Finland, major depression in Han Chinese, and projects to be developed around the Multiethnic Cohort (MEC, composed of Japanese American, African American, Latino American, Native Hawaiian, and European American) as well as the diverse Los Angeles patient population ascertained for eye disease.


Fine-scale demographic history of global populations

One burgeoning movement in medicine is the incorporation of evolutionary principles. Medicine is classically concerned with only the most proximate causes of dysfunction and views diseases as defects of a once perfect machine. In contrast, evolutionary medicine strives to view diseases as the consequence of trade-offs and vulnerabilities due to interactions with our past environments. Therefore, to understand the evolutionary courses of complex traits, we need to understand the demographic history of the population of interest.

To this end, we have investigated the fine-scale structure and history of human populations known to have a unique past or relatively understudied, such as the Sardinians (Chiang et al. bioRxiv 2016, in revision at Nat. Genet.), the Han Chinese (Chiang et al. bioRxiv 2017), and the Finns (Chiang et al. ASHG 2017). Future goals will continue to investigate relatively understudied diverse populations in finer details to shed light on how differential susceptibility to diseases across populations may be rooted in their demographic histories.


Natural Selection and its impact on pattern of variation

Demographic processes such as long-term isolation and bottleneck are not the only forces that shape the pattern of variation in our genome. Natural selection is another important factor that contributes to our genomic pattern of variation and distribution of complex traits. We have conducted genome-wide scans for signals of selection in human populations (Chiang et al. bioRxiv 2017), as well as focusing on the polygenic signature of adaptation of copmlex traits, namely height (Turchin*, Chiang* et al. Nat. Genet. 2012; Zoledziewska*, Sidore*, Chiang*, Sanna* et al. Nat. Genet. 2015). In fact, height is the first robust example of polygenic adaptation in humans, and is now the positive control for developing methods aimed to detect such signatures in other complex traits. We will continue to investigate these imprints left in our genome by Natural Selection, and integrate external, non-genetic, information to contextualize these findings, which may reveal cases where increased susceptibility of certain traits may be a trade-off due to an evolutionary benefit in our past.
